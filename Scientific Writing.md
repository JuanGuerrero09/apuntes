
# Planning and structuring an essay

Steps:
1. Study title and write first outline, look for and evaluate sources
2. Read and make notes (keep record of all sources)
3. Paraphrase and summarize
4. Write draft of main body
5. Write draft introduction and conclusion
6. Rewrite introduction, main body and conclusion, checking for logical development of ideas and relevance
7. Organice list of references, contents, figures and appendices, re check citation
8. Proofread whole essay

NOTE: Citations as in hidrogeolocical journal?

Structure
- Introduction
	- general statement - to introduce subject
	- definitions - to explain any technical terms
	- thesis - to give opinion
	- preview - tell what parts of topic will be included
- Main body: present evidence to support thesis, numbered if possible and ideas order by importance
- Conclusion: summary brief reminder of main ideas and recommendations

Golden Rule of Unity
- One paragraph = one main idea
	- all sentences in the paragraph must be about the same main idea
	- all sentences must be relevant

Sections of paragraph
- Topic sentence (top)
- supporting sentences (middle)
- (possibly) concluding sentence (bottom)

Topic sentence:
- tells what the paragraph is about
- almost always first sentence
- most important in whole paragraph
- divided in
	- **topic** - names the topic
	- *controlling idea* - what the paragraph will say about topic
	- example: **The IUWM approach** *is an important strategy in providing water services of adequate quality*


different patterns for paragraph development
- chronological description
	- Time adverbs and phrases: in 1980 | last week | at 10.15 | first, second, finally | soon | after the project began; • Verb tense sequencing: originally we wanted to… | more recently we have attempted to… | now we are trying to… | in the future we shall try to…; • Grammatical parallelism: mount the grating near the end… | locate a rider on the scale… | adjust the grating… | read the distances on the scale… .
- cause and effect analysis
	- Connective words and phrases: therefore, thus, consequently, accordingly, as a result, so. • Subordinate clauses: since, because (of), due to. • Causative verbs: causes, results in, gives rise to, affects, requires, produces. • Conditional constructions: when, where, given, if, then.
- comparison and contrast
	- Connective words and phrases: however, on the other hand, conversely, similarly, likewise, in contrast to. • Comparative constructions: more than, -er, than, less than, as … as, rather than, is different from. • Verb tense differences: Program X will be easy to implement, whereas Program Y would entail a number of complications. • Subordinate clauses: while, whereas, but. • Parallelism: Model X is reliable and efficient, whereas Model Y is unreliable and relatively inefficient.
- listing
	- Connectives: and, also, in addition, furthermore. • Sequencing: first, second, next, etc. • Parallelism: Model X is reliable and efficient, whereas Model Y is unreliable and relatively inefficient.

concluding sentence
- • Ends paragraph (if long paragraph). • Tells reader that paragraph is finished. • Summarizes main points or links to following paragraph. • Does not introduce new ideas. • Starts with phrase to show end of paragraph, as follows. o In short, . . . o Therefore, . . . o In conclusion, . . .




# Global Datasets in Flood Simulation


Open data is becoming more and more relevant for the water-field practicioners. In hydrology, the precipitation data from space satelites, such as the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission (TRMM) has given the capacity to obtain flood prediction inputs with the advantage of being economical and efficient for researches *(Harris)*. Still, there's room for improvement for the satelite data since it tends to lead to overestimations (*Yang*) that can translade in false alarms for the population  (Moffit). That's where corrections and validatiosn can be introduced in order to improve the results.

Usage of TRMM-based is increasing by it's particular benefits for its usage in remote conditions and diverse conditions. Developing countries are in the specially benefited with the growth of open data satellite missions allowing its access for remote land observation, without having to rely in the more restricted and specialized commercial missions (_Radočaj, D.,_) In particular, TRMM has seen usage in Flood Deterction Systems in countries with complex transboundary management of floods like Bangladesh (*Moffitt*), for flash flood forecasting in arid cities as Riyadh in Saudi Arabia (*Tekeli*) and flood monitoring in humid River Basins in China (*Yang*). The variety of studies show reliability in different enviroments, and has been observed that for modelling of floods on areas where raingall data is not available or existing stations data is insuficcient the TRMM can be used for estimation (*Rahman*)

The reliability of the TRMM data for modelling is not total, it has been observed that  its rainfall data, compared with sources as ECMWF data, posses higher chances of missing floods events (*Rahman*). It has been also observed that the data possesses biases in time and magnitude of the maximum flow rates of the floods and that for some high precipitation events it can lead to oversestimations in the streamflow (*Yang*). Problems in the with false alarms, even for reliable systems with a high detection success, lead to distrust in the population and diminishes the believility of the Flood Detection Systems (FDS) (*Moffit*). Given those different upsides and downsides of the TRMM data, correction and bias adjustment of the precipitation data is suggested as a form to improve form prediction for different flood events (*Harris*)

Correction for the remote sensing data can be done in various forms, such as inclusing physically-based constraints in the model, to improve the accuracy for the ungauged river basins (*Moffitt*). Following with that, it has been observed that rainfall rates and soil moisture are some of the most critical variables in flood forecasting and that including it reduces considerably false floods in the FDS in comparison with data that doesn't consider those parameters (*Tekeli*). Other form of adjustment for the precipitation data is generating correction factors by comparing the TRMM data with gauge data for validation in basins where these are available (*Bhattacharya*). This is more restrictive, since only valid in gauged basins, however, the coefficients used to assess the predictive skill of hydrological models shows improved results after corrected rainfall data for most of the results even though is not observed in all the events analyzed.(*Bhattacharya*).

A brief summary on the benefit of the usage of open data, such as TRMM, can help in flood modelling, however, theres still room for improvement by using different methods that can help in the development of more reliable tools that are transferred as higher quality  instruments for population, increasing flood prevention and trust in the authorities and its systems
