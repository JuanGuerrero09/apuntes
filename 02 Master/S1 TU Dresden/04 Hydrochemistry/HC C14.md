### pe-pH Diagram

General considerations about the occurrence of Fe and Mn

• Under reducing conditions at lower and medium pH values:  Fe2+ (dissolved ions) • Under oxidizing conditions: Fe(OH)3 (solids) • Under strongly reducing conditions and at higher pH values:  FeS, pKsp(FeS) = 18.1 (solids);  • At higher pH values and medium redox intensities: FeCO3, (pKsp(FeCO3) = 10.5, solids;  

The diagram shows
- Reducing conditions (half bottom part)
- Oxidizing conditions (half upper part)
- Basic conditions (half right part)
- Acid conditions (half left part)

• Many elements occur in aquatic systems in different species, whose stability  and transformation is determined by the master variables pe and pH, for  example C, S, N, Fe or Mn. • Species distribution depending                                                                                 of both master variables: f = f(pe, pH) • The graphic illustration of this function   is the borderline between the predominance   areas of the species under consideration.  • For acid/base systems, the borderline is  usually defined by a(base) = a(acid) (f = 0.5).  • For redox systems, it is intended to define a(Ox) = a(Red) analogous to  acid/base-systems. In some cases, this is not possible, because one of the  redox partners does not occur in the liquid phase (e. g. Fe2+/FeOH2(s)). In  such cases, an absolute concentration of the dissolved partner has to be  defined in order to construct the function pH = f (pe).  


#### Properties of pe-pH diagrams

• All borderlines pe = f (pH) are linear.  

• The pe-pH functions are no „sharp“ borderlines, they show only  borderlines between areas where one of the species predominates.  

• For acid/base pairs without any redox reaction, the lines run parallel to  the pe axis (vertical to the pH axis).   pH = pKa 

• Redox systems, not depending on pH,  are characterized by lines,  which are running parallel to the pH axis.    pe = pe0 


#### Stability limits of water

Water as a redox system

1) Reduction of H (ON in H2O: +1) to H2 (ON: 0)    H+ + e- ⇌ 1 2 H2(g) 2) Oxidation of O (ON in H2O: -2) to O2 (ON: 0)   1 4  O2 + H+ + e− ⇌ 1  2 H2O  → 1) and 2) would lead to a decomposition of water, e. g. by electrolysis.        

Finding the stability limits from the pe equations of the decomposition reactions (The maximum partial pressures of H2 and O2 are assumed to be 1 bar.)   


1 4 O2(g) + H+ + e− ⇌ 1 2 H2O lgK = pe0 = 20.75 pe = pe0 + lg a(H+) + 1 4 lg p(O2) with p(O2) = 1 bar: pe = pe0 – pH = 20.75 - pH  


H+ + e− ⇌ 1 2 H2(g) lgK = pe0 = 0 pe = pe0 + lg a(H+) - 1 2 lg p(H2) with p(H2) = 1 bar:     pe = pe0 – pH = -pH 

Water is stable only in a certain range of pe. Consequently, only pe values within the range of stability can occur in natural waters.
