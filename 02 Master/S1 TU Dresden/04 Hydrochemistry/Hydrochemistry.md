## üìò **Water Chemistry (Hydrochemistry)**

---

### üß™ Definition

**Hydrochemistry** is the science that studies the occurrence, properties, and behavior of water constituents in natural systems and within the water cycle.  
It provides **analytical methods** to assess water quality and the **scientific basis** to understand the reactions occurring in aquatic environments.

---

### üí° Hydrochemical Questions

Hydrochemistry seeks to answer questions like:

- How much oxygen (or nitrogen, CO‚ÇÇ, etc.) can be dissolved in water?
- Is a given water analysis **plausible**?
- What **pH** results from dissolving a certain acid, base, or salt?
- Does ammonium exist as **NH‚ÇÉ** or **NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫** at a given pH?
- How to determine concentrations of **CO‚ÇÇ**, **HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª**, and **CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª** using titration?
- What is the role of **inorganic carbon species** in water‚Äôs **buffer capacity**?
- What portion of hardness causes **scaling**, and how to find it analytically?
- What **pH** is needed to **precipitate** a metal as hydroxide?
- Is water in **calco-carbonic equilibrium**, or will **CaCO‚ÇÉ** dissolve or precipitate?
- Can **nitrate** oxidize **sulfide** to **sulfate**?
- Under which conditions does **iron** exist as Fe¬≤‚Å∫ (dissolved) or Fe(OH)‚ÇÉ (solid)?

### Phase transition temperatures

Hydrogen bonds in the **cluster model of liquid water** form almost perfect **tetrahedrons**.  
In liquid water, there is a dynamic equilibrium between **clusters** (molecules connected by hydrogen bonds) and **free molecules**.  
Temperature affects this equilibrium ‚Äî higher temperatures break hydrogen bonds, increasing the number of free molecules.

![[Pasted image 20251020093923.png]]
An increase in **pressure** breaks the tetrahedral structure, forcing molecules closer together and transforming ice into liquid water.

Electrons in smaller atoms are held more tightly to the nucleus. As atom size increases, outer electrons are farther away and held less tightly ‚Äî this affects **polarity** and **bond strength**.

### Water ‚Äì Energetic Quantities

**Enthalpy (H)** is a measure of the total heat content or energy of a system at constant pressure.  
It represents the energy required to create the system and to make room for it by displacing its surroundings.

**Molar enthalpy** refers to the enthalpy change per mole of substance (analogous to using ‚Äúa dozen‚Äù as a unit count in chemistry).

- **Molar enthalpy of fusion (melting):**  
    Amount of energy needed to convert 1 mol of solid water (ice) into liquid water at 0 ¬∞C.  
    ‚Üí ŒîH‚Ççfusion‚Çé = 6.01 kJ/mol at 0 ¬∞C
    
- **Molar enthalpy of vaporization:**  
    Energy needed to vaporize 1 mol of liquid water into gas.  
    ‚Üí ŒîH‚Ççvap‚Çé = 44.02 kJ/mol at 25 ¬∞C,  
    ‚Üí ŒîH‚Ççvap‚Çé = 40.66 kJ/mol at 100 ¬∞C
    

**Molar heat capacity (C‚Çö):**  
Energy required to raise the temperature of 1 mol of a substance by 1 K.

> Water has a very high heat capacity, making it an excellent **medium for heat storage** and temperature regulation.

#### Vapor Pressure of Small Droplets

Small droplets have **higher vapor pressure** than large ones due to their **curvature** ‚Äî smaller radius increases molecular escape tendency.  
This phenomenon is key for **cloud** and **fog formation**.

**Kelvin Equation:**
$$\ln\left(\frac{p}{p_0}\right) = \frac{2 \gamma V_m}{rRT}$$
where:

- p: vapor pressure above a curved surface
- $p_0$‚Äã: vapor pressure above a flat surface
- $\gamma$: surface tension
- $V_m$‚Äã: molar volume of the liquid
- r: droplet radius
- R: gas constant
- T: temperature

### Gibbs Energy (G)

**Gibbs free energy** combines **enthalpy (H)** and **entropy (S)** to determine the spontaneity of a process.
$$
\Delta G = \Delta H - T \Delta S
$$

- If **ŒîG < 0**, the process is **spontaneous**.
- If **ŒîG > 0**, the process is **non-spontaneous**.
- If **ŒîH > 0**, the process is **endothermic**.
- If **ŒîH < 0**, it is **exothermic**.

**Entropy (S)** represents the degree of disorder or randomness in a system.  
**Solvation enthalpy** refers to the energy change when solute particles are surrounded by solvent molecules.

A **dissolution process** runs spontaneously when the total Gibbs energy change (**ŒîG‚Ççsol‚Çé**) is negative.

#### Mass Concentration (Œ≤)

Describes **how much solute mass** is contained in a given **volume of solution**.

$$
\beta(x) = \frac{m(x)}{V} \quad (g/L, mg/L)
$$
_Examples:_
- Coffee contains roughly 100 mg caffeine per 125 mL ‚Üí $\beta = \frac{100\,mg}{0.125\,L} = 800\,mg/L = 0.8\,g/L$

#### Molar Concentration (Molarity, c)

Represents **how many moles** of solute are present per liter (or volume) of solution.
$$
c(x) = \frac{n(x)}{V} \text{ or } \frac{\beta(x)}{M(x)} \quad \quad n(x) = \frac{m(x)}{M(x)} \quad (mol/L, mmol/L)
$$
- $n$: number of moles
- $M$: molar mass (from periodic table), mass per mole
- *m*: mass

_Avogadro-constant_ $(N_A)$ connects the *unified atomic mass unit* with g

$$1u =1.66 √ó 10^{-24}g, \quad 1u√óN_A = 1g‚ãÖmol^{-1}$$
**Definition:**  
A **mole** is the amount of substance containing $6.022√ó10^{23}$ entities (atoms, ions, or molecules).

_Example:_  
Caffeine ‚Üí $M = 194.2\,g/mol$ (from periodic table):

$$
c(x) = \frac{n(x)}{V} = \frac{m(x)/M(x)}{m(x)/\beta(x)} = \frac{M(x)}{\beta(x)}
$$
$$c=\frac{0.8g/L‚Äã}{194.2g/mol}=4.12mmol/L$$

_number of entities_ $(N) = n*N_A$

#### Equivalent Concentration ($c_{eq}$)

Equivalent concentration expresses concentration in terms of **chemical charge equivalents** instead of moles.
$$
c(\frac{1}{z}X) = \frac{n(\frac{1}{z}X)}{V}, \text{ or equivalently }\; c_{eq} = z\,c(x)
$$
Where:
- **c(x)** ‚Üí molar concentration (mol/L).
- **z** ‚Üí ionic charge (e.g., +1 for Na‚Å∫, +2 for Ca¬≤‚Å∫, ‚Äì1 for Cl‚Åª).

This allows comparing solutions based on their **electrical equivalence** rather than their molecular count.

The **condition of electrical neutrality** must always hold:
$$\sum_i c_iz_i=0$$
This means the total positive and negative charges in any solution must balance.

Because all water samples must be electrically neutral, we can verify a water analysis by comparing **cation** and **anion** equivalent concentrations:

Molality measures **moles of solute per kilogram of solvent**, not per liter of solution:

Represents the **proportion of solute mass** relative to total solution mass:

$$

Dimensionless; when multiplied by 100%, gives **mass percent**.

#### Hardness of Water

- **Total Hardness (TH):** sum of $Ca^{2+}$ and $Mg^{2+}$ concentrations.
- **Carbonate Hardness (CH):** part of hardness due to $HCO_3^-$‚Äã and $CO_3^{2-}$‚Äã.
- **Non-Carbonate Hardness (NCH):** remainder due to other anions (e.g., $SO_4^{2-}‚Äã, Cl^-$).


$$
TH = CH+NCH
$$

Only $Ca^{2+}$ and $Mg^{2+}$  determine hardness.

---

When **CH < TH**, both carbonate and non-carbonate hardness are present.  
When **CH = TH**, only carbonate hardness exists (no non-carbonate part).  
CH can **never exceed** TH.

![[Pasted image 20251027103330.png]]
At high temperatures, **temporary (carbonate) hardness** causes precipitation of calcium carbonate ‚Äî the main reason for **scaling in pipes and boilers**.

#### Why is carbonate hardness _temporary_?

Carbonate hardness is mainly caused by **calcium bicarbonate** ($Ca(HCO_3)_2$) and **magnesium bicarbonate** ($Mg(HCO_3)_2$), which are **soluble salts**.  
When water containing these ions is **heated**, the bicarbonates **decompose**, releasing **carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ)** and forming **insoluble carbonates** that precipitate:

$$Ca(HCO_3)_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 \downarrow + CO_2 \uparrow + H_2O$$

This means that when water is boiled or evaporated, part of the dissolved calcium and magnesium are removed from solution as **solid calcium carbonate (CaCO‚ÇÉ)** or **magnesium carbonate (MgCO‚ÇÉ)**.  
Because this hardness can be **removed by heating**, it is called **temporary hardness**.

In contrast, **non-carbonate hardness** (from ions like $SO_4^{2-}$‚Äã or $Cl^-$) remains even after boiling ‚Äî it is **permanent hardness**.

This process explains the formation of **scale in kettles, boilers, and pipes**, where calcium carbonate accumulates as a solid layer.

---
How to calculate it from the $HCO^{-}_3$?
$$
c(Ca^{2+}+Mg^{2+})_{CH}=\frac{1}{2}c(HCO^{-}_3) 
$$
This relationship arises from **charge balance** in the bicarbonate system.

Each **calcium ion** (Ca^{2+}) or **magnesium ion** (Mg2+Mg^{2+}Mg2+) carries **two positive charges**, while each **bicarbonate ion** (HCO3‚àíHCO_3^-HCO3‚àí‚Äã) carries **one negative charge**.  
Therefore, to maintain electrical neutrality, **two bicarbonate ions** are required for every **one divalent cation**:

$$Ca^{2+} + 2HCO_3^- \rightarrow Ca(HCO_3)_2$$

Hence, the **concentration of divalent cations (Ca¬≤‚Å∫ + Mg¬≤‚Å∫)** associated with carbonate hardness is **half** the concentration of bicarbonate ions:

$$c(Ca^{2+}+Mg^{2+})_{CH} = \frac{1}{2}c(HCO_3^-)$$

This proportionality reflects the **stoichiometry** of the ions in solution and ensures **charge neutrality** between cations and anions in the carbonate equilibrium system.

##### Worked example (cleaned and explained)

**Given (one water sample):**

- $c(Ca^{2+}) = 1.8\ \text{mmol/L}$
- $c(Mg^{2+}) = 0.4\ \text{mmol/L}$
- $c(HCO_3^-)=2.6\ \text{mmol/L}$

**Step 1 ‚Äî Total hardness (TH):**  
Total hardness is the sum of the alkaline-earth cation concentrations (as mmol/L here):

$$TH=c(Ca^{2+})+c(Mg^{2+})=1.8+0.4=2.2¬†mmol/L$$

**Step 2 ‚Äî Carbonate hardness (CH):**  
Carbonate hardness is the portion of Ca¬≤‚Å∫ and Mg¬≤‚Å∫ that is paired with bicarbonate/carbonate. Stoichiometry requires 2 bicarbonate ions per one divalent cation, so the carbonate-associated cation concentration is half the bicarbonate concentration:

$$CH = \tfrac{1}{2}\,c(HCO_3^-) = \tfrac{1}{2}\times 2.6 = 1.3\ \text{mmol/L}$$

Interpretation: up to 1.3 mmol/L of the Ca¬≤‚Å∫+Mg¬≤‚Å∫ present can be associated with bicarbonate (i.e., ‚Äútemporary‚Äù hardness).

**Step 3 ‚Äî Non-carbonate hardness (NCH):**  
Non-carbonate hardness is the remainder of TH not accounted for by CH:

$$NCH = TH - CH = 2.2 - 1.3 = 0.9\ \text{mmol/L}$$

Interpretation: 0.9 mmol/L of the Ca¬≤‚Å∫+Mg¬≤‚Å∫ are associated with other anions (e.g., SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, Cl‚Åª) ‚Äî this is **permanent hardness** (won‚Äôt precipitate by simple heating)- .

##### What happens if we have high hardness? (practical consequences & remedies)

**Phenomena / problems:**

- **Scaling / fouling** in boilers, kettles, heat exchangers, and pipes (CaCO‚ÇÉ deposits reduce efficiency).
- **Poor soap performance:** hard water reacts with soaps to form insoluble ‚Äúsoap scum,‚Äù reducing cleaning efficiency.
- **Plumbing & appliance damage:** scale narrows pipes and shortens appliance lifetime.
- **Increased energy consumption:** scale lowers heat transfer efficiency.
- **Aesthetic/operational issues:** spots on dishes, dull laundry, taste changes.
- **Possible effects on corrosion:** hard water can be less corrosive in some systems (protective scale), but scale formation can cause under-deposit corrosion in others.

**How to mitigate high hardness:**

- **Ion exchange (water softening):** replace Ca¬≤‚Å∫/Mg¬≤‚Å∫ with Na‚Å∫ (common domestic softeners).
- **Lime softening:** add Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ to precipitate CaCO‚ÇÉ and Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ in water treatment.
- **Reverse osmosis / nanofiltration:** remove hardness ions physically.
- **Sequestration / chelants (polyphosphates):** keep hardness ions in solution to prevent precipitation (temporary measure, not removal).
- **pH control / CO‚ÇÇ management:** in industrial systems, control pH/CO‚ÇÇ to minimize scale formation.
- **Descaling maintenance:** chemical or mechanical removal of accumulated scale.

Water hardness quantifies the concentration of **alkaline earth ions**, mainly calcium ($\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$) and magnesium ($\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$). Hard water influences technical processes, domestic applications, and aquatic chemistry through scaling and changes in pH.

### Ionic strength, activities

Ionic solutions do not behave ideally because ions **interact electrostatically**. Oppositely charged ions tend to cluster, forming an **ion atmosphere**.

![[Pasted image 20251110095758.png]]

These interactions modify the ‚Äúeffective‚Äù concentration of ions, which is described using **activity**.
#### Activities
Activity represents effective concentration:

$$a_c = \gamma_cc
$$
Where:
- $a_c$ = activity
- $c$ = molar concentration
- $\gamma_c$ = activity coefficient
Limiting behavior:
- At **infinite dilution**, interactions vanish
- $\gamma \to 1$ as $c \to 0$
#### Ionic Strength

Ionic strength is a measure of the total concentration of ions, weighted by charge:

*Estimation of ionic strength*:
$$\text{Exact: } \quad I=0.5\sum c_iz_i^2$$
Charges strongly influence $I$ because they appear squared.
$$\text{Approximationusing conductivity: } \quad I(mol/L)=\frac{k_{25}(\text{in mS/m})}{6200}$$
This is helpful when only bulk conductivity is known.

#### Calculation of Activity Coefficients

##### Debye‚ÄìH√ºckel Equation (valid for $I < 0.005$ mol/L)

For **univalent ions**:
$$\log \gamma_z = -0.51\sqrt{I}$$
For **multivalent ions**, deviations grow large, requiring extended forms of the Debye‚ÄìH√ºckel equation:

For polyvalent ions (charge = z):

## Reaction Equilibria in Aquatic Systems

### Law of Mass Action (LMA)

The law quantifies the equilibrium position for a reaction:

$$
K^* = \frac{(a_c)^{v_c} (a_D)^{v_D}}{(a_A)^{v_A}(a_B)^{v_B}}
$$

Where:

- $\nu$ = stoichiometric coefficients
- $K^*$ = thermodynamic equilibrium constant
- $a$ = activities of species

A **large** $K^*$ indicates that products dominate at equilibrium.

High K -> more products than reactants 

---
#### Rules of LMA

- Products ‚Üí numerator
- Reactants ‚Üí denominator
- Stoichiometric coefficients ‚Üí exponents

Special cases:
- **Solids**: concentration is constant ‚Üí activity = 1  
    (concentration fixed by molar density)
- **Solvents (e.g. water)**: present in large excess ‚Üí activity ‚âà 1  
    (‚âà55.56 mol/L), excluded from equilibrium expression
- **Gases**: described using partial pressure instead of concentration

Example:
$$\mathrm{CaCO_3(s)} + \mathrm{CO_2(g)} + \mathrm{H_2O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Ca^{2+}} + 2\ \mathrm{HCO_3^-}$$
The equilibrium expression becomes:
$$K = \frac{a_{\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}}\cdot a_{\mathrm{HCO_3^-}}^2}{p_{\mathrm{CO_2}}}$$
Because CaCO‚ÇÉ(s) and H‚ÇÇO(l) are omitted.

---

#### General Reaction Notes

- Reverse reaction constant: $1/K$ or $1/K^*$
- Combined equilibria multiply:

$$K_1^* K_2^* = \frac{a_C}{a_A a_B}$$

Activities must always be used for thermodynamic consistency.

###  Colligative Properties

Colligative properties are properties of solutions that depend solely on the **number of solute particles** in a solvent ‚Äî _not on their identity_.  
This means that adding 1 mol of NaCl or 1 mol of glucose affects the solvent similarly if they both contribute the same number of dissolved particles.

Main colligative properties:

- Vapor pressure lowering
- Freezing point depression
- Boiling point elevation
- Osmotic pressure

---
#### üîπ Vapor Pressure Lowering

The **vapor pressure (VP)** of a pure liquid is the pressure exerted by its vapor when in equilibrium with its liquid phase 
When a **non-volatile solute** (like salt) is added, **some surface water molecules are replaced by solute particles**, meaning fewer water molecules can evaporate.  
This results in a **lower vapor pressure**.

For **non-dissociated solutes**, Raoult‚Äôs law gives:

$$
x_2 = \frac{p_{01} - p_{1}}{p_{01}}= \frac{\Delta p}{p_{01}}
$$
where

$$
x_2= \frac{n_2}{n_1+n_2}
$$
- $p_{01}‚Äã$: vapor pressure of pure solvent
- $p_1$: vapor pressure of solution
- $x_2$‚Äã: mole fraction of solute
- $n_1, n_2$‚Äã: moles of solvent and solute, respectively

‚û°Ô∏è This relation is valid **only for non-dissociated solutes**, i.e. substances that remain as single molecules in solution (no ionic splitting).

---

#### üîπ van‚Äôt Hoff Factor $(i)$

When solutes **dissociate into ions**, each formula unit produces more particles.  
The **van‚Äôt Hoff factor $i$** accounts for this:

$$
i = 1 + (v-1)\alpha
$$

where:

- $v$: number of ions formed per solute molecule (e.g. NaCl ‚Üí 2)
- $\alpha$: degree of dissociation (fraction between 0 and 1)

If fully dissociated ($\alpha = 1$), then $i = v$.

Thus, for dissociating solutes:


$$
x_2= \frac{i\cdot n_2}{n_1+i\cdot n_2}
$$

---

##### Example ‚Äì CaCl‚ÇÇ in Water

**Question:** What is the relative lowering of vapor pressure if 1 mol of CaCl‚ÇÇ is added to 1 L of water? Given:

- M_{H2O} = 18 \, \text{g/mol}
- \rho = 1 \, \text{g/cm}^3
- \alpha = 1

$$\begin{gather}n_{H2O} = \frac{1000}{18} = 55.56 \text{ mol}, \quad i = 3 \\
\frac{\Delta p}{p^0} = \frac{i n_2}{n_1 + i n_2} = \frac{3(1)}{55.56 + 3(1)} = 0.0513\end{gather}$$

‚û°Ô∏è **Relative lowering:** 5.1 %

**Note:**  
‚Äú**Dissociates**‚Äù means the solute splits into ions in water.  
$\text{CaCl}_2 \rightarrow \text{Ca}^{2+} + 2 \, \text{Cl}^-$‚Üí 3 total particles, so it reduces vapor pressure more than a non-dissociated solute.

---
#### üîπ Phase Diagram Explanation

In the phase diagram, adding a solute shifts the equilibrium lines:

- The **vapor pressure curve** moves downward because solute particles reduce the escaping tendency of solvent molecules.
- The **freezing point** decreases ‚Äî the system requires a lower temperature for solid‚Äìliquid equilibrium.
- The **boiling point** increases ‚Äî the liquid needs more heat to reach a vapor pressure equal to the external pressure.

Thus, the **liquid phase region broadens** between the freezing and boiling points.

#### üîπ Freezing Point Depression

When solute is added, ions disrupt ice crystal formation, making freezing more difficult.

For **non-dissociated solutes**:
$$
\Delta T_f = bK_{Kr}
$$

For **dissociated solutes**:
$$
\Delta T_f = i\cdot b \cdot K_{Kr}
$$

General form:
$$
\Delta T_f =  K_{Kr}\sum_i b_i
$$
where:

- $K_{Kr} = -1.86 \, \text{K¬∑kg¬∑mol}^{-1}$ (cryoscopic constant for water)
- $b$: molal concentration
- $i$: van‚Äôt Hoff factor

Hence, if 1 mol solute is added to 1 kg of water, the freezing point lowers by **1.86 K** (for ideal, non-dissociated solutes).

---

#### üîπ Boiling Point Elevation

Analogous to freezing point depression, but in the opposite direction:

$$\Delta T_{b} = i \, b \, K_{eb}$$

where  
$K_{eb} = 0.513 \, \text{K¬∑kg¬∑mol}^{-1}$

Adding solute raises the boiling point since the vapor pressure must reach atmospheric pressure at a higher temperature.
#### Example

**Given:** $b = 2 \, \text{mol/kg}, \, i = 2$

$$\begin{gather} 
\Delta T_b = 0.513 \times 2 \times 2 = 2.05 \, K \\
\Delta T_f = -1.86 \times 2 \times 2 = -7.44 \, K
\end{gather}
$$

---
#### üîπ Endothermic Dissolution ‚Äî Beer Cooling Example üç∫

When **salt (NaCl or CaCl‚ÇÇ)** is added to ice, it lowers the freezing point so the ice melts.  
Melting requires **absorbing heat** from the surroundings (endothermic), cooling nearby bottles.  
CaCl‚ÇÇ is more efficient because each molecule produces **3 ions**, enhancing the effect.

#### üîπ Osmosis and Osmotic Pressure

**Osmosis**: movement of solvent through a **semipermeable membrane** into a solution with higher solute concentration.  
The pressure required to stop this flow is the **osmotic pressure ($\pi$)**.
![[Pasted image 20251103205157.png]]
>Works for gases as well

For non-dissociated solutes:
$$\pi = cRT$$
For dissociated solutes:
$$\pi = i \, cRT$$
General form:
$$\pi = RT \sum_i c_i$$
where:

- $\pi$: osmotic pressure
- $R = 0.083145\, \text{bar¬∑L¬∑K}^{-1}\text{¬∑mol}^{-1}$
- $T$: absolute temperature (K)
- $c$: molar concentration
---
#### üîπ Reverse Osmosis

If **external pressure** > osmotic pressure, solvent is forced out of the solution ‚Äî used in **desalination** and **purification** systems.
![[Pasted image 20251103205527.png]]
Higher salt concentration ‚Üí higher osmotic pressure ‚Üí more pressure required.

#### üîπ Osmotic Coefficient

In non-ideal solutions, real osmotic pressure is lower than ideal predictions.  
The **osmotic coefficient ($\theta$)** expresses this deviation:

$$\theta = \frac{\pi_{real}}{\pi_{ideal}}$$

For seawater with 35 g/kg:  
$\theta = 0.90$ (0 ¬∞C) ‚Äì $0.91$ (30 ¬∞C)
