## Vulnerability - Concepts and Analyses

$$\text{Risk = hazard * (exposure) * \textbf{vulnerability} (related to the hazard)}$$
$$\text{Vulnerability = value/function * susceptibility * coping capacity}$$

Hazard: **Probability** and features of **events** with the potential to result in harm
Risk: **Probability** of  negative social, economic and ecological consequences) 
Vulnerability: Physical, social,  economic, ecological and institutional **values** or **functions**, susceptability and coping capacity) 

### Concepts - vulnerability : areas 

**Value**: The importance worth or usefulness of something
**Susceptibility**: The state or fact of being likely or liable to be influenced or harmed by a  particular thing”
**Coping capacity**: Ability to “deal effectively with something difficult”

### Assessment of vulnerability

Vulnerability divided in:

**Direct** (physical water contact): buildings - infrastructure - crop, cattle - capital goods - consumer goods 

**Indirect** (no physical water contact): life - health - environment - unique goods - art and  cultural  goods 

and

**Tangible** (monetary): production  losses (in and  outside inundation area) - market  discturbances - loss of time

**Intangible** (non-monetary): reduced competitiveness - migration - increased vulnerability of  economic actors 


### Ecological flood vulnerability

Biomechamical vulnerability: Typical forces generated by flooding over a plant cultivar and respective vulnerabilities  

Physiological vulnerability: Sub-systems of the flood-plant interrelations and the related vulnerabilities of soils

### Social flood vulnerability

Indicators: Household composition , health conditions, employment, insurance

Study methods: Participant observation, interviews, focus groups

### Related concepts 

Adaptability: Ability of a system to **alter its processes and functions** (efficiently and fast)  to changed circumstances

Transformability Capacity **to create a fundamentally new system** when boundary conditions  make the existing system untenable (Walker et al. 2004) 

Resilience System’s ability to **retain characteristic elements, structures and processes** and reorganise while undergoing change;

#### Engineering resilience of buildings:

a) Flood protection: Defence structure around the building to prevent flood  water approaching the building and its surrounding 
b) Flood resistance: Construction of the building to prevent flood water  entering the building and damaging its fabric 
c) Flood resilience: Construction of the building to minimise water impacts  and to facilitate fast recovery (e.g. drying, cleaning)  without remaining damage

### Flood damage analysis 


Influencing factors of flood damage

Hazard
	- Hydrological load
		- Flow velocity
		- Flood duration
		- Water level
	- Contamination

Susceptibility
	- Permanent susceptibility
		- Structural provisions
		- Type of building
	- Temporal susceptibility
		- Flood warning
		- Preparedness

Plain floods
- slow increase and decrease of the water  level; flow velocity of less than 1 m/s 
- Consequences: moisture damage, water damage, contaminated water damage, hydrostatic damage by pressure and  upwelling because of rising groundwater 

Flash floods
- flow velocity higher than 1 m/s in catchments with steep slopes
- consequences: statical (foundations) damage, damage by crashing loads, hydrostatic damage, moisture damage, water damage 

### Types of damages at flooded buildings

- Type 1: Moisture damage / water damage: with any flow, varies depending on the construction material, capillarity water absorption
- Type 2: Statical damage
- Type 3: Contamination damage

#### Moisture damage

- reason: infiltration of surface or groundwater, backwater from sewer system
- consequences: efflorescence of salt, moisture expansion of wooden part, corrosion, mould
- factors: altitude of surface water and groundwater level, duration of those water levels


Ways of moisture absorption of building components:
- Capillary water absorption
- Water vapour diffusion into pore
- Hygroscopic absorption 

Moisture behaviour of timber
- decreases compresive, tensile and banding strength
- possibility of damage of material with fungi, algae and pest
- expansion and volume swelling as consequences

Moisture behaviour of brickwork 
- Water absorption in the pores and capillaries of the material
- Tension and cracks appear because water absorption

Methods of moisture measurement 
- Surface-ball-Electrode 
- Gravimetric moisture measurement   

#### Statical damage

- Group a) Foundation damage
	- Reason: undercutting of foundations, cohesive soil platification
	- consecuences: partial collapse, foundation laying open, crack damage and deformation
	- factors: stresm velocity, water level
- Group b) Damage caused by hydrostatical pressure and crashing flotsam 
	- reason: Hydrostatic pressure on external  walls, Punctual load because of  crashing flotsam
	- Consequences: collapse, deformation, crack damages
	- Factors: stream velocity, crashing floatsam, groundwater and floodwater level
- Group c) Overloaded building components 
	- reason: Overload of supporting  components (superior  permanent load because of  capillary absorbed water) 
	- consequences: superior deflection, crecks at supporting components, deformations
	- Factors: flood water level, saturation moisture

#### Contamination damages
- reasons: heating oil in flood water, toxicological chemical substances in flood water, biological contamination (from WWTP)
- consequences: oil in building component, smell, deposit of contaminated mud
- factors: contamination level of flood water, flood water level, duration of the flood water level

### Reactive and precautionary measures

Moisture damages: lower water level, recondisioning, drying
Statical: statically saving with supports, deconstruction of non-repairable components, restore load capacity
Contamination: deconsturction of contaminated components, machanical and chemical purification

### Derivation of depth-damage functions 

Approach:
- Building taxonomy -> determination of representatives -> synthetic flooding in stages -> damage types of flooded buildings (three types) -> definition of repair measures -> quantity determination and valuation -> **depth-damage function**

Flood-damage simulation model:
- Flood hazard (flooding + gw inundation) + Exposure (analysis of flood prone areas) + Vulnerability (determining potential impacts due flooding) => Calculation  (calculating damage cost) => **Flood risk** (current/future flood risk as risk maps)

## Risk Analysis and Evaluation

**Task and components of flood risk management**

- Risk analysis: Hazard analysis, vulnerability, risk determination
- Risk evaluation: Evaluation of risk, evaluation of risk reduction
- Risk reduction: Pree flood, flood event and post-flood risk reduction

### Purpose of risk analysis

Purpose of risk analysis: determine flood risk as an **objective** quantitative and/or  qualitative term of **probable negative consequences**. 

Risk analysis in unprotected areas: probability of high water levels and their negative consequences

Risk analysis in protected areas: determine the probability of **failures of flood defenses** and  their negative consequences. 


#### Hazard Map (Suisse concept)

Intensity vs Probability

Intensity:
- Strong: $d > 2m$   or $v*d > 2m²/s$
- Medium $2m > d > 0.5m$  or $2m²/s > v*d > 0.5m²/s$
- Low: $d < 0.5m$  or $v*d < 0.5m²/s$

Probability:
- High: 1-20 (1-50 yrs)
- Medium 30 -100 (100 - 200) yrs 
- Low > 100 (300 and more) yrs 

(in parentesis the new european directive)

### Relativity of flood risk 

Risk vs risk perception = Risk is the probability of negative consequences vs cognition of risk based on cultural and personal context

Risk evaluation: judgement on risk by a person or a group

### Terms and basic concepts of risk evaluation 

Evaluation: systematic investigation and evaluand worth and merits
Ex-ante evaluation deals with a future state of the evaluand, ex-post evaluation with a previous state

Requirements for risk evaluation: Utility - Feasibility - Probriety - Accuracy

Major terms:
- Indicator: Quantitative or qualitative representative
- Index: aggregated term based on a set of parameters or indicators  
- Threshold: Targeted value of an indicator
- Weight: Coefficient of importance of indicators
- Evaluation concepts: Rule on which an evaluation or decision is based on

**Indicators of risk evaluation**
- Hydrological/hydraulic indicators: max discharge and max water depth
- Socio-cultural indicators: loss of life
- Economic indicators: direct economic loss
- Ecological indicators: abundance of benthic algae
- Integral indicators (index): overall multiple lost

Concepts of risk evaluation
- Tolerability: risk as low as reasnable
- Risk aversion: biased perception based on negative consequences
- Effectiveness: degree of goal achievement
- Efficiency: degree of economically achievement of effects
- Sustainability: long term social, economic and ecological conditions

### Mono-criteria evaluation methods 

Effectiveness is determined by the degree of goal  achievement.

**Cost-effectiveness (CET)** compares the relative expenditure  (costs) and outcomes (effects) of actions.

**efficiency: benefit-cost ratio’** Benefit-cost ratio considers both benefits and costs in  monetary terms. 

**Robustness**: Performance of alternative measures or instruments under  different (future) conditions. 

Strengths and weaknesses of evaluation  concepts ‘effectiveness’ and ‘efficiency’ :
- Strengths: discrete (tangible results), transparent calculation
- Weaknesses: restriction to intended effects (effectivemeness), limitations to monetised and tanglible effects only 

### Multi-criteria evaluation (MCE) methods

Major steps of MCE:
- 1. Impact analysis (assignment of criteria + individual evaluation)
	- Determination of relevant indicators/criteria
	- Definition of targets/thresholds
	- Calculation for individual indicators
- 2. Equity analysis (assignment of weights + weighing/ranking)
	- Adjustment of weigths for each indicator/criterion
	- Final evaluation

#### Relevant approaches

**Multi-Attribute Decision Making (MADM)**
- Choosing from small, finite or contable number of alternatives
- Methods: Compromise Programming, Hasse Diagram Technique

**Multi-objective decision making (MODM)**
- Choosing from a large, infinite or uncountable number of alternatives
- Methods: Linear Goal Programming

## Scenarios

### Scenarios as foresight approach 

Scenario: assumption about the future or possible future situation as well as the pathway to that future situation

Methods for dealing with the future: Prognosis, prospective model simulations, scenarios, visioning

**Types of scenarios** (variable -> scenario type):

- Direction of time view -> Forecasting and backcasting
- Independence -> explorative and normative
- Intervention -> Baseline and policy
- Participation -> top down and bottom up
- Likelihood -> Deterministic and probabilistic
- Number -> Alternatives vs one current trend

**Major types of scenarios in decision making**

- Explorative scenarios: involve extrapolation, forecasting and projections
- Normative: scenarios: Describe a future situation that is aspired or should be avoided
- Vision or policy scenarios: cover intended or policy actions


**Qualitative and quantitative scenario types**

Qualitative (focus on narrative) - Quantitative (Focus on numerical values)

Crossed with

Participatory approaches - Analytical approaches 

### Scenario development 

In:
- Natural sciences - output orientated
- Social sciences - process orientated
- Environmental sciences - combination

#### Storylines

- Key element of scenarios, describe narratively different developments and the relationships between them.
- They refer to systems elements prone to change
- Grounded through participation of stakeholders

### Scenario planning 

Scenario development: Focuses on possible change of driving forces of system elements
Scenario analysis: Investigates effect of the changing driving forces
	- Cross-section analysis
	- Longitudinal section analysis

**Principal components of scenario planning**:
- Phase 1: Delineation of the system
- Phase 2: Identification of drivers or factors
- Phase 3: Projection of drivers or factors

1. Delineation and description of the system

## Land Use as Part of  Integrated Flood Risk Management 

### Understanding land cover (LC) and land use (LU) 

**Land cover (LC)**: Observable physical and biological cover of the earth's land, as soil, vegetation or human-made structures, directly observable and determined b natural conditions and land use

**Land use (LU)**: Total of objectives, processes and structures of human's arrangements and activities on the land surface, including pressures on and dependencies from nature, has societal and physical dimension, not entire directly visible
- Land use connects the human and natural system

### Classification of LC/LU 

LC and LU can be classificated depending on thematic focus, levels and number of classes, spatial resolution, data  format, metadata, availability, actuality and so forth 

Land cover: CORINE (Europe), ATKIS (Germany)
Land use: No consistent approach

#### Data sources for classification of LC and LU

- Land cover (LC)
	- Satellite images
	- Aerial photographs
	- Topographic maps
	- Field surveys
	- Central databases
- Land use (LU):
	- Field Surveys
	- Statistics
	- Model calculations
### Interrelations of LU with the water balance 

Land use is understood as all human-made interrelations with the  water balance. To describe the interrelations between land use and the water balance all relevant hydrological processes are identified. 

**Process-based system analysis** 

Process (Interception) -> Subprocess (Evaporation, wetting, etc) -> LU Parameter (Coverage canopy/shrub level, interception maximum) 

Open space **rural** land use water-related **parameters**:
- Height canopy/shrub level 
- Coverage main vegetation species
- Interception maximum
- Evapotranspiration crop/vegetation coefficient
- Main soil characteristics
- Irrigation
- Mean albedo

Urban land use water-related parameters:
- Roof area density
- Roof material
- Density open level
- Water consumption
- Interception maximum
- Urban soil
- Evapotranspiration

**Spatial land-use parameter analysis**

Land-use types (LUTs) are based on the land cover but are specified by their characteristics, values can be projected together with LUTs

Generation of LUTs:
- Land cover ingormation
- Correlation between land cover and water-related parameter values 

GIS is used to allocate the parameter values that show a correlation to  the LUTs.

### Retrospective analysis and projection of future LC/LU change 

Any LC projection need analysis of retrospective LC change

LC projections refer to
- spatio-temporal allocation of LC Classes
- **demand** for land-cover classes

Methodological steps
- I) Retrospective analysis
- II) Allocation of land-cover classes change
- III) Projection of demand for LC classes

Land-use parameter values are projected in two principal ways:  
	(i) Projection according to the land-cover change
	(ii) Projection of individual parameter values

Projection considers:
- The land cover
- site-specific natural conditions
- the change of individual parameters values
### Including the LC/LU change in a parameter model 

To include land-use projections into (coupled) water and matter balance  models, a consistent set of parameters is needed to ensure the same parameters for each model 

Coupled modelling for system analysis. Each land use type have land-cover type and land-use parameters

## Measures and Instrument for Risk Reduction 

### Flood risk and risk reduction 

#### Source-pathway-receptor consequence concept

- Source (Rainfall, wind, waves)
- Pathway (overtopping, overflow, floodplain inundation)
- Receptor (property, people, environment)
- Consequence (loss of life, stress, material damage, environmental degradation)

##### Reduction coefficients

$$
FR = f((p,m,\textbf{t})_{source},(i,a,\textbf{f})_{pathway},(s,\textbf{r})_{receptor},(v,c,\textbf{d})_{consequence})
$$
being:
- p = probability, m = magnitude, **t = retention** 
- i = inundation, a = attributes, **f = protection/defense** 
- s = susceptibility, **r = resistance resp. resilience**
- v = value or function, c = coping capacity, **d = decrease or compensation**

#### Pre-flood risk reduction
- Long-term intervention
- Form of intervention: Prevention, control, protection, mitigation, preparedness
- Example: Dams

#### Flood event risk reduction
- Short-term intervention
- Form of intervention: Defense, warning and emergency response
- Example: Emergency response

#### Post-flood risk reduction
- Short-term intervention
- Form of intervention: Recovery
- Example: toxicological investigations

### Measures and instruments – terms and categories 

Measure: Intervention causing impacts _directly_ ex. dike
- Traditional flood protection
- Visible implementation
- Can be expensive with considerable side effects

Instrument: Intervention triggering mechanism which lead to impacts _indirectly_ ex. land-use plan
- Indirect approaches
- Often cheaper than measures
- May have limited side effects
- More flexible

#### Classification

**Measures**
- Adaptative measures: Direct biophysical interventions into natural structures (source, pathway) 
	- Land management
	- River channel and coastal management
- Control measures: Introduction, alteration or removal of built structures (source,  pathway, receptor) 
	- Flood storage
	- Flood water transfer
	- Coastal alignment
- Retreat measures: Temporal or permanent relocation of elements and uses at risk (receptor)
	- Evacuation of human life
	- Evacuation of asset and life stock
	- Relocation of uses

**Instruments**
legislation and politics on European,  national and state level not considered
- Regulation instruments: Prescriptive regulations of land uses and behaviour (source, pathway, receptor) 
	- Water management designations
	- Spatial planning designation
	- Nature protection designations
- Stimulation instruments: Financial incentives or disincentives (source, pathway, receptor) 
	- Financial incentives
	- Financial disincentives
- Compensation instruments: Distributing of the risk resp. the burden of occurred financial losses  within a wider community (consequences) 
	- Risk and loss distribution
- Information instruments Provision of information / warning / instructions for stakeholders of  flood risk management (source, pathway, receptor) 
	- Information / Dissemination
	- Warning / Instruction

## Legal Framework of Flood Risk Management

- International Law

### International Law

- Sources of international law: International conventions, international custom and general principles of law
- Customary international law: Principles of territorial sovereignty and integrity and principle of good neighbourliness

- Regional conventions: International commission for the protection of the Elbe (and other rivers)

- Primary legislation or **treaties** are effectively the constitutional law
	- Foundation of everything the EU does
	- Created by governments of all EU states
- Secondary legislation: 
	- Regulations (without requirement for any implementing measures)
	- Directives (achieve certain result)
	- Decisions

### EU Floods Directive
Has 3 instruments of flood risk management
- Preliminary flood risk assessment
- Flood maps for areas 
- Flood risk management plants

Public consultantion: Member States  shall make available to the public  the preliminary flood risk assessment,  the flood hazard maps, the flood risk maps and  the flood risk management plans.”
